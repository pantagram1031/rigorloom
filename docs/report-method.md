# What makes a defensible student research report

This is the generic methodology the pipeline builds toward. It is
provider-neutral and persona-free: it describes the shape of a good report, not
any individual's voice or direction. Voice and direction, when they apply, come
from the operator's private preference packs at runtime.

## The report is a diagnosis of real student work

The pipeline organizes, verifies, and typesets a student's own investigation. It
does not invent the topic, the data, or the results. If the substance is missing,
ask for it rather than fabricate it — self-directed inquiry is the point, and
manufactured content defeats it.

## Eight-element structure

A defensible report carries these elements, and spends more words on *why*,
*how*, and *what next* than on the bare result:

1. **Motivation / background** — a coursework unit or concept leads to a
   question; the storyline does not break away from what was actually studied.
2. **Question / purpose** — one narrow, concrete question with clear variables
   and scope. Giant abstract topics are a weakness, not a strength.
3. **Theoretical background** — start from the course concept, then extend one
   step deeper, with real sources.
4. **Method / process** — reproducible procedure, and the *reason* each method
   was chosen.
5. **Results** — tables and figures with a first-pass interpretation, carried
   through to what the data *means*.
6. **Conclusion / implications** — an actual answer to the question, not a
   summary.
7. **Limits / follow-up** — honest limits plus at least one concrete next
   question.
8. **Reflection / growth** — specific changes in thinking, method, or skill.

## Load-bearing rules

- **Reproducible deterministic core.** The heart of the report is a simulation or
  computation anyone can re-run. Its output is the evidence; prose describes it
  but never replaces it.
- **Anti-fabrication.** Every result number is produced by a re-runnable
  computation or is explicitly marked as the student's own analysis. No number
  appears in the report that cannot be regenerated. To change a number, change
  the inputs and rerun — never hand-edit a result.
- **Honest error / limits axis.** A report that measures something must report
  its error, its assumptions, and where it breaks down. The limits section is
  first-class, not an afterthought.
- **Reflection separate from results.** Keep interpretation and personal growth
  out of the results section. First-person voice belongs to motivation and
  reflection; the analytical body stays impersonal and declarative.
- **Literature comparison.** Situate the work against similar cases or prior
  studies — what matches, what differs, what is genuinely the student's own view.
- **Real citations only.** Cite real papers and books. Do not invent sources,
  authors, or page numbers. Keep source clutter out of the body text; move it to
  provenance or endnotes.

## The epistemics rule (toy model vs. real system)

A small model demonstrates a *principle*; its specific numbers do **not** transfer
to the real system it is inspired by. State plainly what the model shows and what
it does not. Do not present a toy result as a measurement of the real world, and
do not soften a measured result into vague tendency language, nor strengthen an
inference into "always / must / required."

## Level and scope discipline

Match the concept level to the reader: explain a term's functional meaning or
drop it, rather than name-dropping advanced machinery for effect. Keep the
question narrow enough to answer with the evidence at hand. A short summary
deliverable, when required, is a faithful compression of this same report —
motivation, process, result/growth, and follow-up — never a place to introduce
claims the full report did not support.
